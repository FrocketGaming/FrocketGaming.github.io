F1 Score

Classification

The harmonic mean of precision and recall, providing a balanced measure of the model's performance.

Formula:

F1 = 2 \cdot \frac{Precision \cdot Recall}{Precision + Recall}

Pros:

  • Balances precision and recall
  • Useful for imbalanced datasets

Cons:

  • Doesn't account for true negatives
  • May not be suitable when false positives and false negatives have very different costs

ROC AUC

Classification

Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic curve, measuring the model's ability to distinguish between classes.

Interpretation:

AUC of 0.5 indicates random guessing, while 1.0 indicates perfect classification.

Pros:

  • Insensitive to class imbalance
  • Provides a single score for model comparison

Cons:

  • May be too optimistic for highly imbalanced datasets
  • Doesn't provide information about model calibration

Mean Squared Error (MSE)

Regression

The average of the squared differences between predicted and actual values.

Formula:

MSE = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2

Pros:

  • Penalizes larger errors more heavily
  • Always positive, making it easy to interpret

Cons:

  • Sensitive to outliers
  • Not in the same unit as the target variable

R-squared (RÂ²)

Regression

The proportion of the variance in the dependent variable that is predictable from the independent variable(s).

Formula:

R^2 = 1 - \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \bar{y})^2}

Pros:

  • Easy to interpret (ranges from 0 to 1)
  • Indicates how well the model fits the data

Cons:

  • Can increase with the addition of irrelevant variables
  • Doesn't indicate whether the model's predictions are biased

Mean Absolute Error (MAE)

Regression

The average of the absolute differences between predicted and actual values.

Formula:

MAE = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n |y_i - \hat{y}_i|

Pros:

  • Less sensitive to outliers than MSE
  • In the same unit as the target variable

Cons:

  • Doesn't penalize large errors as heavily as MSE
  • May not be suitable when large errors are particularly undesirable

Log Loss (Cross-Entropy Loss)

Classification

Measures the performance of a classification model where the prediction is a probability value between 0 and 1.

Formula:

LogLoss = -\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n [y_i \log(\hat{y}_i) + (1 - y_i) \log(1 - \hat{y}_i)]

Pros:

  • Penalizes confident misclassifications more heavily
  • Suitable for multi-class classification

Cons:

  • Can be sensitive to outliers
  • Less intuitive to interpret than accuracy
